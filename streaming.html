The Rise of Streaming

By: Javie T. Alfaro

In the past couple of years, the entertainment industry has gone through a massive shift that has changed the landscape of various forms of media. Traditional TV and cinema have massively declined in favor of more modern and alternative platforms. The rise of streaming services such as Netflix, Disney+, Amazon, Hulu, and many others has changed the way people consume media. Revolutions are nothing new, we have seen its impact on various other industries before. This shift is part of the greater Digital Revolution, a force that, like many before it, has brought both progress and disruption. Seeing all the new changes in the traditional system, we must now analyze its’ consequences and find a good course of action to follow.

The Decline of Traditional Media

Before the rise of streaming, television and movie theaters ruled the entertainment industry. They traditionally followed strict rules and rigid formulas that most TV networks and studios followed. Audiences were used to scheduled programming and conventional varieties of film. Genres such as sitcoms and late-night talk shows ruled television up until the 2000s. Most TV shows followed a seasonal format with 20+ episodes each. Meanwhile, movies typically follow a theatrical run, then a release on DVDs, and eventually cable TV.

However, with the decline of DVDs and physical media, the economic model of filmmaking has changed drastically. These days, sitcom and late-night shows all are largely dying and TV shows now usually have an average of 12 episodes per season. This issue was highlighted by actor Matt Damon in an interview on Hot Ones, noting that the fall of DVD sales has made studios less willing to invest in original, character-driven films. Instead, they favor large franchises that promise greater financial return. As a result, creative risk-taking in mainstream cinema has significantly diminished.
The Streaming Takeover

The convenience of streaming services has largely been the root cause of these changes. There is no doubt that media is more accessible than ever before. These platforms have become a hub for experimental storytelling and international content. This was shown in the rise of new and non-english content such as Money Heist and Squid Game achieving global success. Moreover, writers and filmmakers have been able to receive much greater creative freedom to pursue more diverse projects. As a result, underrepresented groups and voices have been able to breach film and pop culture in general.

Despite all of these positive changes, not all aspects have been quite as smooth sailing. For one thing, the accessibility of entertainment has driven studios to continuously produce new content in much shorter time frames. The pressure to constantly release new content has led to an oversaturated market, where many shows and films feel repetitive or formulaic. Quality is often sacrificed for quantity, resulting in numerous reboots and sequels driven more by profit than creativity. Furthermore, the rise of many different streaming companies has lead to the decline of syndication. Subscription models now dominate, which incentivizes companies to exclusively lock their content in their own platforms. Consequently, users are now forced to juggle many platforms if they want to view their favorite shows and movies.

The Future

Syndication, once a mainstay of television distribution, has largely disappeared. This limits the long-term reach of many shows and restricts content availability. As the industry continues to evolve, it’s clear that we must weigh the benefits of innovation against the cost of losing traditional strengths. Streaming is here to stay with all its groundbreaking changes and exciting opportunities. The challenge lies in ensuring that the future of entertainment remains both accessible and creatively rich, while maintaining the essence of what filmmaking really is about.
